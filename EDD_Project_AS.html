Element A - Problem Identification and Justification

Problem Verification

Why countertops became our focus

Element A establishes the problem, validates who is affected, and explains why this project matters. The research connects countertop cleanliness to hygiene, time efficiency, and mental stress.

Problem Statement

Dirty countertops can negatively impact hygiene, organization, preparation areas, and can take up a significant amount of time to clean. In a study conducted by the journal Mindfulness, clean surfaces were linked to a decrease in anxiety of about 27%. In addition, a 2017 study by The National Library of Medicine emphasized the importance of hygiene by finding that S. Aureus — a bacteria which could lead to pneumonia and bloodstream infections — was found on countertops and refrigerator door handles in 39% of households. Furthermore, according to Wallstone USA and Onix Cleaning Services, 30–45 minutes a day is recommended for deep cleaning countertops, and even longer may be required when dealing with stubborn stains. These issues related to cleanliness, health, and time consumption could all be addressed through a solution focused on improving countertop sanitation and maintenance.

The team examined existing cleaning products, patents, and automated systems. Many products improve manual cleaning or automate floors, but they do not fully solve autonomous countertop cleaning.

B1 Previous Solution Attempts

Manual pads and squeegee tools

Multi-surface cleaning systems

Automated table or floor cleaning devices

Design gap found

1.0 Brainstorming Possible Solutions

The team considered manual devices, stationary cleaning tools, and autonomous robotic systems. The strongest concepts were the ones that reduced user effort while still remaining realistic to build with available parts.

D.1 Concept Development

The chosen concept developed into a mobile robot with wheels, a cleaning attachment, and sensors for ledge and obstacle detection.

Mobility

Motorized wheels move the robot across a flat surface.

Cleaning Contact

A sponge or brush wipes the surface while the robot moves.

Safety

Sensors detect ledges and obstacles to prevent falls.

D.2 Concept Analysis

Concepts were compared by efficiency, safety, ease of use, cost, size, and cleaning ability. The circular body offered compact turning potential, the tread-style concept explored stronger transport, and the rectangular reservoir concept provided the clearest layout for storing components and contacting the countertop.

D.3 Solution Selection

The final selected concept was a fully autonomous countertop cleaning robot because it best reduced user effort and met the project goals. The selected direction combined the most practical parts of the CAD concepts: autonomous movement, a simple cleaning attachment, compact organization, and sensor-based safety.

Element I - Testing, Data Collection, and Analysis

Results and Evaluation

What testing revealed about the prototype

The prototype was partially successful: sensors and cleaning contact worked, but movement and turning control needed more improvement.

I1.0 Test and Evaluate the Prototype

In the first testing round, sensors worked but the robot moved too quickly and sometimes jumped. After revisions, speed was reduced, wait functions were added, and movement became safer. In the second testing round, the robot was no longer in danger of falling, but turning control still needed improvement.

First Testing

About 29–33 seconds per column; sensors worked, but speed and turns were inconsistent.

Revisions

Speed was reduced, wait functions were added, and the goal was narrowed to one cleaning column.

Second Testing

About 30–32 seconds per column; safer movement and better cleaning contact, but turning issues remained.

Final evaluation: the concept is possible, but the next version should improve drivetrain settings, balance, turning accuracy, and cleaning path structure.

Next-Step Recommendations

  • Improve VEXcode drivetrain settings for accurate turns.
  • Reduce turning speed separately from forward speed.
  • Move the battery and brain closer to the center.
  • Keep the sponge/brush attachment because it was practical.
  • Measure future results using coverage, turning angle, edge distance, and repeatability.
