The Countertop Conundrum

Executive Overview

A smarter way to approach countertop cleaning

This portfolio follows the development of an autonomous countertop-cleaning robot from problem research to prototype testing. The project focuses on reducing the time, stress, and hygiene concerns caused by dirty countertops while documenting the engineering decisions that shaped the final prototype.

Introduction and Problem Justification

Problem Verification

Why countertops became our focus

This section explains the real-world need behind the project. The problem is not just that countertops get dirty; it is that the cleaning process affects hygiene, time management, kitchen organization, and stress for the people who use the space every day.

Problem Statement

Dirty countertops can negatively impact hygiene, organization, preparation areas, and can take up a significant amount of time to clean. In a study conducted by the journal Mindfulness, clean surfaces were linked to a decrease in anxiety of about 27%. In addition, a 2017 study by The National Library of Medicine emphasized the importance of hygiene by finding that S. Aureus — a bacteria which could lead to pneumonia and bloodstream infections — was found on countertops and refrigerator door handles in 39% of households. Furthermore, according to Wallstone USA and Onix Cleaning Services, 30–45 minutes a day is recommended for deep cleaning countertops, and even longer may be required when dealing with stubborn stains. These issues related to cleanliness, health, and time consumption could all be addressed through a solution focused on improving countertop sanitation and maintenance.

HealthCountertop surfaces can become a point of contact for bacteria and food-preparation contamination.
TimeManual deep cleaning takes repeated effort and can become difficult for busy households.
StressDirty or cluttered surfaces can make a kitchen feel less organized and harder to use.
NeedAn automated solution could improve consistency while reducing the amount of manual labor required.

Documentation and Analysis of Prior Solution Attempts

Prior Solution Attempts

What already exists, and why our design is different

The team examined existing cleaning products, patents, and automated systems. Many products improve manual cleaning or automate floors, but they do not fully solve autonomous countertop cleaning.

Previous Solution Attempts

Manual pads and squeegee tools

These tools improve hand cleaning but still rely completely on user effort.

Multi-surface cleaning systems

These systems help apply cleaning solutions but still require people to control the process.

Automated table or floor cleaning devices

Some devices show that automation can work, but they are not designed for everyday elevated countertops.

Design gap found

A compact robot is needed to clean countertop surfaces, avoid edges, and reduce user effort.

Presentation and Justification of Solution Requirements

Requirements and Proposal

Turning the problem into measurable goals

The project requirements translate the problem into measurable performance targets. These criteria helped the team judge whether the prototype was successful, what data needed to be collected, and which limitations still needed to be improved.

Design Specifications

The robot should clean efficiently, navigate safely, remain practical for a kitchen environment, stay compact, avoid collisions, and be easy to maintain.

Clean within a reasonable timeTimed runs in seconds/minutesShows whether automation actually saves user effort.
Avoid ledges and obstaclesSensor activation and pass/fail observationsPrevents falls, damage, and unsafe operation on elevated countertops.

Project Proposal

The proposed solution was an autonomous countertop cleaning robot that moves across a countertop, uses sensors to avoid ledges and obstacles, and applies a sponge, brush, or pad to clean the surface.

Brainstorming Possible Solutions

The team considered manual devices, stationary cleaning tools, and autonomous robotic systems. The strongest concepts were the ones that reduced user effort while still remaining realistic to build with available parts.

Concept Development

The chosen concept developed into a mobile robot with wheels, a cleaning attachment, and sensors for ledge and obstacle detection.

Mobility

Motorized wheels move the robot across a flat surface.

Cleaning Contact

A sponge or brush wipes the surface while the robot moves.

Safety

Sensors detect ledges and obstacles to prevent falls.

Concept Analysis

Concepts were compared by efficiency, safety, ease of use, cost, size, and cleaning ability. The circular body offered compact turning potential, the tread-style concept explored stronger transport, and the rectangular reservoir concept provided the clearest layout for storing components and contacting the countertop.

Solution Selection

The final selected concept was a fully autonomous countertop cleaning robot because it best reduced user effort and met the project goals. The selected direction combined the most practical parts of the CAD concepts: autonomous movement, a simple cleaning attachment, compact organization, and sensor-based safety.

The team revised the build because battery and brain placement affected balance, and early motor behavior caused jumping or galloping. The construction process became a cycle of building, noticing a mechanical or coding issue, revising the structure, and testing again.

Prototype Testing and Data Collection Plan

Testing Plan

How the team planned to measure success

Testing focused on whether the robot could move safely, detect edges, detect obstacles, maintain cleaning contact, and complete a controlled cleaning run. The plan used both quantitative data and qualitative observations.

Test Criteria and Methodology

Criteria included cleaning time, ledge detection, object detection, no damage, size and weight limits, and visual cleaning improvement. The team planned repeated trials so results could be compared instead of relying on one run.

MetricData typeMethod
Run timeQuantitativeTime each run in seconds/minutes.
Ledge sensor performanceQualitative and quantitativeRecord successful and missed sensor activations.
Obstacle responseQualitativeObserve whether the robot detects and reacts to objects.
Cleaning effectivenessQualitativeInspect whether the cleaned area looks visibly improved.

Test Procedure

Setup

Place the robot on a clear surface and check battery, code, and cleaning contact.

Run

Start the timer and record movement, sensor activation, and cleaning contact.

Repeat

Run multiple trials and repeat with changes or obstacles.

Analyze

Compare time, movement, sensor performance, and cleaning to the criteria.

Testing, Data Collection, and Analysis

Results and Evaluation

What testing revealed about the prototype

The prototype was partially successful: sensors and cleaning contact worked, but movement and turning control needed more improvement.

Test and Evaluate the Prototype

In the first testing round, sensors worked but the robot moved too quickly and sometimes jumped. After revisions, speed was reduced, wait functions were added, and movement became safer. In the second testing round, the robot was no longer in danger of falling, but turning control still needed improvement.

First Testing

About 29–33 seconds per column; sensors worked, but speed and turns were inconsistent.

Revisions

Speed was reduced, wait functions were added, and the goal was narrowed to one cleaning column.

Second Testing

About 30–32 seconds per column; safer movement and better cleaning contact, but turning issues remained.

Final evaluation: the concept is possible, but the next version should improve drivetrain settings, balance, turning accuracy, and cleaning path structure.

Next-Step Recommendations and Limitations

  • Improve VEXcode drivetrain settings for accurate turns.
  • Reduce turning speed separately from forward speed.
  • Move the battery and brain closer to the center to improve balance.
  • Keep the sponge/brush attachment because it was practical.
  • Improve sensor placement so the robot can better detect ledges and obstacles.
  • Revisit the original spray-system idea only after movement and sensor reliability are solved.
  • Measure future results using coverage, turning angle, edge distance, and repeatability.
